Offensive Systems
11-4-1 SetPrimary
Most common NCAA offense. A1 at X behind goal, A2/A3 on wings at GLE, M1 up top, M2/M3 on wings high. Creates isolation dodging and 2-man game opportunities. Ball starts at X or up top.
Used by: Virginia, Duke
22-3-1 SetPrimary
A1 at X, A2/A3 on crease, M1/M2/M3 spread across the top. Strong for dodging middies from up top. Crease players create traffic and scoring opportunities inside.
Used by: Maryland, Hopkins
33-3 SetBalanced
A1/A2/A3 low around the cage, M1/M2/M3 high across the top. Balanced look good for motion offense, cutting patterns, and ball movement. Equal threat from all positions.
Used by: Syracuse
4Invert OffenseMismatch
Attack plays up top (normally midfield positions), middies play behind the cage (normally attack positions). Forces defensive mismatches — long poles covering smaller attackmen up top, short sticks behind.
Used by: Notre Dame
5Motion OffenseFlow
Continuous cutting, screening, and ball movement. No set plays — read the defense and react. Players interchange positions, creating confusion. Requires high lacrosse IQ and stick skills.
Used by: Most college programs as base concept
62-Man GameCore Action
Pick/roll action between two players. Ball carrier dodges from up top (island) while adjacent player sets a pick. Read the switch: if they switch, roll to goal. If they trail, turn the corner. Primary modern college action.
Used by: Every NCAA program — foundational action
7West Genny OffenseTempo
Quick ball movement drill turned offense concept. Ball moves rapidly around the formation — X to wing to top to opposite wing. Fast-paced, forces defense to rotate constantly. Shoot when the slide is late.
West Genesee style — high-tempo passing
8EMO 3-3 SetMan-Up
Extra man offense: 3 up top (T1/T2/T3), 3 around crease (C1/C2/C3). Read the slide — when defense commits, move the ball to the open man. Quick ball movement forces 5 defenders to cover 6.
Video: POWLAX EMO 3-3
9Fast Break OffenseTransition
4v3 or 3v2 transition advantage. Push tempo immediately after saves, turnovers, or face-off wins. Ball carrier attacks, fill lanes wide, look for early shots. Don't force it — if no shot, settle into half-field offense.
Key: Push within 5 seconds of possession
10Stall / Delay OffenseLate Game
Late game possession strategy. Spread to 4 corners with 1 behind and 1 up top. Control tempo, burn clock. Only attack on high-percentage looks. Force defense to chase — draw penalties.
Used when: Leading late, clock management situations
Defensive Packages
1Adjacent SlidePrimary
First slide comes from the nearest (adjacent) defender to the ball. Most common college slide package. Adjacent defender is "hot" — responsible for stopping the dodge. 2-slide fills from crease or backside.
Standard at every level of men's lacrosse
2Crease SlidePrimary
Crease defender slides first to the ball carrier, regardless of position. Others rotate to fill crease. Creates a longer slide but keeps perimeter defenders on their men. Good vs. strong dodging teams.
Common at youth/high school levels
3Backer SlideSituational
Defender behind/below the dodger slides up to meet the ball. Used primarily against top-side dodges. The backer approaches from behind — ball carrier doesn't see the slide coming. Effective vs. island dodgers.
Used vs. elite top-side dodgers
4Double SlideAggressive
Two defenders slide aggressively to the ball, creating a trap. Leaves backside open — requires remaining defenders to rotate quickly. High risk, high reward. Forces turnovers against less skilled ball handlers.
Situational — use against weaker offensive teams
5Zone Defense (3-3)Zone
Area-based defense — 3 defenders high, 3 low. Protect the middle of the field and the crease. Defenders guard zones, not men. Effective against teams that rely on dodging. Vulnerable to quick ball movement and shooting from outside.
Brine zone / 3-3 zone concept
6Man Down: Box and 1Man Down
5 defenders total: 4 in a box formation protecting the crease area + 1 chaser who pressures the ball. Box rotates to ball side. Chaser denies easy looks and disrupts offensive flow. Used when opponent has one dominant shooter.
Video: POWLAX Box and 1
7Man Down: House ZoneMan Down
5 defenders protect "the house" — the high-danger area in front of the goal. Zone-based: rotate to the ball, collapse inside. Give up outside shots, deny crease and close-range looks. Strong vs. teams that feed the crease.
Video: POWLAX House Zone
8Man Down: 5-Man RotationMan Down
5 defenders rotate continuously toward the ball. As ball moves, the entire unit shifts. Every player takes a turn being "top" and "crease." Requires communication and conditioning. Denies clean looks everywhere.
Video: POWLAX 5-Man Rotation
Ride Systems
110-Man RideFull Press
Full pressure ride — every player has a named assignment. Ball carrier gets immediate pressure. Adjacent players deny outlets. Midfielders seal off midfield. High-energy, forces turnovers in the clearing end. Requires elite conditioning and discipline.
Used by: Virginia, UNC, Loyola • Video: POWLAX 10-Man Ride
23-3 Deep Zone RideZone
Zone-based ride: 3 players high (around the clearing team), 3 players deep (protecting midfield). Don't chase the ball — guard zones and passing lanes. Forces the clear into the middle where help is waiting. Lower energy than full press.
Video: POWLAX 3-3 Zone Ride
3Windshield Wiper RideShift
Defenders shift side to side based on ball position — like a windshield wiper. When ball is on the right, the entire ride unit shifts right. Forces the clear to reverse field, buying time. Effective against slow clear teams.
Good for: Teams with athletic midfielders
4Goalie RideTriggered
Ride is triggered by the goalie's call after a save. Goalie reads the clear and calls the ride based on what he sees. If goalie sees a vulnerable clear, he calls "FIRE" for full press. If not, calls "RELEASE" to get back on defense.
Key: Goalie communication is critical
5Zero RidePassive
No ride — get back and set up half-field defense immediately. Used when protecting a lead, managing energy late in the game, or when the opponent has an elite clearing team. Sprint back, get matched up, and play defense.
Used when: Protecting a lead, tired team, elite clearing opponent
6Press RideAggressive
Full-field man-to-man pressure on every clearing player. Every defender matches up and denies the outlet. Extremely aggressive — high turnover potential but risky if beaten. Requires elite athletes and conditioning.
High risk/reward — use sparingly
7Midfield TrapTrap
Force a double-team at the midfield line. Let the clear advance, then trap the ball carrier at midfield with two defenders. Creates a 10-second call situation and forces bad passes. Effective against teams that clear through one player.
Target: The primary clear outlet player
8Contain RidePassive
Slow the clear without pressuring hard. Defenders stay in passing lanes but don't attack the ball. Goal is to burn time off the clock and force a slow, methodical clear. Good for end of quarters or when leading and managing tempo.
Used when: Managing clock, end of quarter situations
Clear Systems
12-3-2 ClearPrimary
New standard college clear formation. 2 defenders behind, 3 midfielders across the midfield area, 2 attack high. Goalie outlets to a defender, ball moves through midfield. Multiple outlet options in every direction.
Video: POWLAX 2-3-2 Clear
21-4-1 ClearSpread
Spread formation: 1 behind (goalie or defender), 4 across midfield area, 1 high. Creates maximum spacing and forces the ride to cover the entire width of the field. Good against zone rides — find the gaps.
Best against: Zone ride coverage
3Break ClearFast
Fast outlet up field — push tempo immediately after a save or turnover. Goalie makes quick outlet to breaking midfielder. Attack leaks out early. Goal: advance ball past midfield in under 4 seconds. Creates transition numbers advantage.
Key: Goalie read speed is everything
4Goalie ClearGoalie
Goalie carries the ball himself past midfield. Used when ride overplays the outlets. Athletic goalies can dodge past the first rider and advance. Risky but effective when the goalie is a strong ball handler. Creates an instant numbers advantage.
Requires: Athletic, confident goalie with stick skills
5Wheel ClearPatient
Ball rotates through the defense — goalie to D1, D1 to D2, D2 to D3, then look for the outlet. "Wheel" the ball around the back until the ride shifts and an outlet opens. Patient clear, burns clock, but safe against aggressive rides.
Used when: Ride is aggressive and cutting off direct outlets
6Reverse ClearCounter
Switch the field against pressure. When the ride overloads one side, reverse the ball quickly to the opposite side of the field. Skip pass across the back or quick wheel to the weak side. Exploits over-aggressive rides.
Key: Recognizing the overload quickly
7Quick ClearImmediate
Immediate outlet after a save — no wheel, no setup. Goalie catches and throws to the first open player. Fastest clear option. Catches the ride before they set up. Requires outlets to be in position before the save.
Key: Pre-save positioning by midfielders
8Press Breakvs. Press
Clear designed specifically to beat a full press ride. Creates pre-planned picks and screens in the clearing end. Designated "breaker" gets free using off-ball movement. Ball handler has a progression read: breaker first, then wheel, then goalie carry.
Video: POWLAX Press Break
Face-Off Playbook (Boys Only)
Face-Off Techniques
TechniqueDescriptionBest Used When
ClampCover the ball with the stick head, trap it, and rake it out. Most common technique at all levels.Default move — use as primary technique
PlungerPush the ball forward off the whistle using a plunging motion. Quick and direct.Against slow opponents or when you have a speed advantage
MotorcycleTwist the stick handle like a motorcycle throttle while pulling back. Creates torque to win possession.Against strong opponents who win the clamp battle
Over-the-TopGo over the opponent's stick to clamp on the far side. Surprise move.Change of pace — use sparingly to catch opponent off guard
Wing Positioning
Wing players line up on the wing lines, even with the face-off X. On the whistle, wings release to compete for ground balls. Left wing should pinch toward the FOGO. Right wing seals off the opponent's wing. Win the ground ball battle on the wings.
Face-Off Plays & Tactics
Pre-Face-Off Reads:
• Watch opponent's hand position — high grip = clamp, low grip = plunger
• Watch their stance — wide base = power, narrow = speed
• Talk to wings about where you plan to direct the ball

Post Face-Off Transition:
• Win Clean: FOGO scoops and immediately looks to push transition. Wings fill lanes.
• Win Dirty (Ground Ball): Wings and FOGO converge on loose ball. Box out, scoop through.
• Lose Clean: Sprint back to defensive end. Match up immediately.
• Lose Dirty: Contest the ground ball. If you can't win it, slow them down for defense to set.
Video: POWLAX Face-Off Fundamentals
Face-Off Personnel
RoleDescriptionWhen to Use
FOGOFace-Off Get-Off specialist. Takes the draw, wins it, subs off immediately for a midfielder. Pure specialist.When you have a dominant face-off man who isn't a strong midfielder
Two-Way MidTakes face-offs AND stays on as a midfielder. Saves a substitution, plays both ways.When your face-off guy is also a strong midfielder
Wing SpecialistDesignated wing player with elite ground ball skills. Wins the 50/50 balls on the wing line.Every face-off — always put your best ground ball guys on wings
Technique Selection Strategy
vs. Clamp-Heavy Opponent: Use motorcycle or over-the-top to counter their clamp. Go under or around.

vs. Speed Opponent: Use a power clamp. Get low, get wide, and overpower them at the X.

vs. Power Opponent: Use plunger or quick exit. Win with speed — get the ball out to wings fast.

vs. Unknown Opponent: Start with your best move (likely clamp). Adjust after 2-3 face-offs based on what you see.

Late Game / Must-Win: Go with your highest-percentage move. No experiments. Win the possession.
